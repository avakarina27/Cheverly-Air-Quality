Health & Air Quality FAQs

This guide explains the Cumulative Exposure Air Quality Index (CE-AQI), a pilot tool developed by the Cheverly EJAT to track the combined health risks of multiple pollutants.

The CE-AQI Explained

The primary difference between the CE-AQI and the traditional EPA index is that the CE-AQI covers high-priority pollutants for overburdened areas like Cheverly. It accounts for cumulative exposure from mobile, stationary, and industrial sources across the region.

The CE-AQI is built on solid science, adapting the traditional index to include toxic components like Black Carbon and Diesel Particulate Matter (DPM).

What pollutants are measured?

Diesel Particulate Matter (DPM) & Black Carbon

Commonly known as "soot," these are fine particles released by diesel engines (trucks, buses, trains). These particles are small enough to enter the bloodstream and are linked to significant respiratory and cardiovascular risks.

PM2.5 (Particulate Matter)

Extremely small particles (2.5 microns or smaller) from smoke, dust, and industrial exhaust. PM2.5 is the most widely tracked indicator of poor air quality.

Ozone, NO2 & CO

Gaseous pollutants primarily generated by vehicle traffic and chemical reactions in sunlight. These can irritate the lungs and trigger asthma attacks.

CE-AQI Health Risk Categories

Category Index Value Health Description
Good 0 to 50 Air quality is satisfactory; little or no risk.
Moderate 51 to 100 Acceptable; risk for unusually sensitive people.
Unhealthy (Sensitive Groups) 101 to 150 Sensitive groups (children, elderly, asthmatics) may experience health effects.
Unhealthy 151 to 200 The general public may begin to experience health effects.
Very Unhealthy 201 to 300 Health alert: increased risk for everyone.
