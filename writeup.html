How the Network Was Built

Since 2007, community members from Cheverly, Fairmount Heights, Seat Pleasant, and Cedar Heights have been concerned about health risks due to heavy diesel traffic and neighboring industries. These include diesel bus maintenance, concrete batching, metal shredding, and recycling operations.

Beginning in 2018, the Center for Community Engagement, Environmental Justice, and Health (CEEJH) at UMD established the PurpleAir sensor network. By 2024, the addition of five C-12 monitors began detecting very high Black Carbon emissions. In 2025, Quant-AQ Modulair sensors were added to measure Ozone, NOX, and Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs).

Current Network Stats:
  • ~30 PurpleAir Monitors (PM2.5)
  • 5 Black Carbon C-12 Monitors
  • 5 Quant-AQ Modulair Sensors (Ozone, CO, NOX)
  • Deployed strategically with UMD Center for Atmospheric and Oceanic Sciences

Why the CE-AQI?

After years of frustration being told by the EPA that air quality was "Good" because only a small set of pollutants were considered, the EJAT developed its own technique. Traditional AQI tells people the air is healthy when, because the highest-risk pollutants (like Black Carbon) are ignored, it is most likely unhealthy.

"Perfection should not be the enemy of the good. Say nothing is irresponsible. EJAT believes using the traditional AQI to communicate health risks in overburdened communities is inappropriate."

The CE-AQI is a creative tool that uses solid science to communicate the complex mixture of pollutants being measured in our community.

Step-by-Step Calculations

1. Calculate Pollutant-Specific AQIs

  • PurpleAir: Calculate 24-hour AQI for PM2.5 using the 24-hour NAAQS. Also calculate an Annual AQI using the long-term average.
  • Quant-AQ: Calculate AQIs for Ozone, NOX, and CO where available.
  • C-12: Calculate a Black Carbon AQI (Pilot index).

2. Identify the Base & Adders

We use a "Base + Adder" approach to account for cumulative burden:

Step A: The pollutant with the highest score becomes the Base Value.

Step B: Add conservative percentages ("Adders") for each additional pollutant:

  • AQI 80-99: Add 1% of Base
  • AQI 100-119: Add 2% of Base
  • AQI 120+: Add 3% of Base

Who Is Taking Action?

The community, with assistance from UMCP and MDE, has established a community-based workgroup that meets every two weeks to address air pollution sources like Sheriff Road, Route 50, and Kenilworth Avenue.
